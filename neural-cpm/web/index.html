What am I looking at?

The closed curve is the “surface”. Around it we lay a narrow band of grid cells. Each band cell remembers which curve point is closest to it — that is the closest-point map.

Diffusion on the curve is faked by alternating two cheap moves on the grid: copy values along the closest-point map (extend), then take a standard 2-D heat step (heatStep). Bilinear interpolation (pullToCurve) brings the new values home.

Use the radio buttons above to isolate each stage visually. The same three stages reappear in 3-D below, where the neural operator replaces the grid extension.

Part 2 — neural CPM on a 3-D surface (the Apple)

In 3-D, building a regular grid + closest-point map for an arbitrary mesh is fiddly and dense. The neural variant replaces step ① with a learned rot_update that smooths a function defined near the surface in a way that respects the surface geometry. Steps ② & ③ then become a sparse linear solve (Poisson) or a sequence of explicit Euler steps (Heat).

The viewers below show precomputed outputs from the notebook ncpm_poisson_and_heat.ipynb. You can rotate, zoom, and pan with the mouse; step through the Poisson pipeline; and play the Heat animation.
