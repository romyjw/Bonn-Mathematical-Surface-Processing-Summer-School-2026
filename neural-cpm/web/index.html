First, an interactive 2-D Closest-Point Method demo so you can see the algorithm; then a 3-D viewer of the same algorithm running on the Apple surface, with the neural extension replacing the classical band extension.

What am I looking at?

The closed curve is the “surface”. Around it lies a narrow band of grid cells. Each band cell stores the nearest point on the curve — the closest-point map.

Diffusion on the curve is approximated by alternating: copy \(u\) along the closest-point rays (extend), then apply a standard 2-D heat step on the grid (heatStep). Bilinear interpolation (pullToCurve) brings values back.

Visualisation modes

  • curve only — the curve, no colour data
  • closest-point map — arrows to nearest curve points
  • band cells — grid cells inside the band
  • after extend — \(u\) copied into band (constant along each ray)
  • heat-step Δ — increment \(\Delta u\) after grid heat sub-steps
  • after pull-back — updated \(u\) restricted to the curve

Part 2 — neural CPM on a 3-D surface (the Apple)

In 3-D, building a regular grid + closest-point map for an arbitrary mesh is fiddly and dense. The neural variant replaces step ① with a learned neural extension that smooths a function defined near the surface in a way that respects the surface geometry. Steps ② & ③ then become a sparse linear solve (Poisson) or a sequence of explicit Euler steps (Heat).

The viewers below show precomputed outputs from the notebook ncpm_poisson_and_heat.ipynb. You can rotate, zoom, and pan with the mouse; step through the Poisson pipeline; and play the Heat animation.

Poisson — \(\Delta_S\, u = f\) on the Apple

With \(f(x,y,z) = \sin(\omega x)\sin(\omega y)\sin(\omega z)\), \(\omega = 10\), and zero-mean gauge \(\textstyle\int_S u = 0\).

The linear system solved on the band is \(L\,u_{\mathrm{band}} = \delta x^2\, f_{\mathrm{smoothed}}\), where \(L\) is the 7-point stencil without the \(1/\delta x^2\) factor.
